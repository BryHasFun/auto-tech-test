GENERAL DATA & SPECIFICATIONS PAGE — 1960s–2000 Automotive Technology

Use this page during the test. The goal is to see whether the applicant can read, apply, and reason from technical information.

Basic electrical
• Ohm’s Law: Volts = Amps × Ohms. Amps = Volts ÷ Ohms.
• A fully charged 12V battery at rest is commonly about 12.6V.
• Typical charging voltage on many 12V vehicles is roughly 13.5–14.7V at fast idle. Always verify vehicle-specific specs.
• Voltage drop testing should be done with the circuit operating under load.
• As a general diagnostic target, high-current circuits should have very low voltage drop. A ground-side drop around 0.8V on a headlamp circuit is suspicious and should be investigated.
• Corroded grounds can cause dim lights, backfeeding, false readings, no-crank conditions, and intermittent electrical faults.
• Relays allow a low-current control circuit to switch a higher-current load such as fans, fuel pumps, horns, and lighting.

Mechanical/engine
• Healthy gasoline engines commonly show about 17–21 inHg manifold vacuum at warm idle, but camshaft, altitude, tune, and engine condition matter.
• Low steady vacuum can suggest late ignition timing, valve timing issues, restricted exhaust, intake leaks, or poor engine condition.
• Compression readings should generally be reasonably even. A common rule of thumb is that cylinders should be within about 10–15% of each other, but always verify specs.
• Two adjacent low cylinders can suggest a head gasket leak between cylinders or another localized sealing problem.
• Backfire through the carburetor can be caused by incorrect firing order, ignition timing, valve timing, lean condition, or intake valve sealing issues.
• Torque values and tightening sequences protect parts and gaskets from distortion and uneven clamping.

Brake system
• Pascal’s Law: pressure applied to a confined fluid is transmitted equally through the fluid.
• Brake fluid is intended to transmit force hydraulically; air in the system compresses and can cause a spongy pedal.
• A sinking brake pedal with no external leak can suggest internal master cylinder bypass.
• Pulling under braking can be caused by uneven friction, hydraulic restriction, contaminated linings, seized hardware, tire/suspension issues, or side-to-side brake imbalance.
• Disc/drum systems may require proportioning, metering, and residual pressure control depending on the setup.
• Drum brakes require proper shoe adjustment and working self-adjusters to keep pedal travel correct.

Cooling system
• A pressure cap raises the boiling point by increasing system pressure. Many later systems use caps around 13–16 PSI, but older/custom systems vary.
• A 50/50 coolant/water mix commonly provides freeze protection, corrosion protection, and improved boiling protection compared with plain water.
• Overheating at idle often points toward airflow problems: fan, shroud, fan clutch, electric fan wiring/control, or blocked airflow.
• Overheating at highway speed may point toward flow or heat-transfer problems: restricted radiator, collapsing hose, water pump issue, lean mixture, ignition timing, or airflow restriction.
• A thermostat stuck open can cause slow warm-up and poor heater performance. A thermostat stuck closed can cause rapid overheating.
• Combustion gases entering the cooling system can cause rapid pressure buildup, coolant loss, bubbles, overheating, and sometimes white exhaust smoke.

Safe shop habits
• Verify the complaint before replacing parts.
• Use the correct service information for the exact year, make, model, engine, transmission, and option package.
• On custom builds, document modifications before diagnosis: added EFI, ignition boxes, electric fans, relays, fuses, grounds, fuel pumps, brake conversions, and cooling changes.
• When uncertain, test first. Parts-cannon diagnostics are expensive and hide the real cause.
